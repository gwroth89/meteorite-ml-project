    Project Summary
    The goal of our project was to learn whether we could predict the likelihood that a meteorite was seen as it fell rather than being found without visual confirmation. Then, taking the additional step to identify what features made it more likely that a meteorite would be seen falling.
    Modelling
    We trialed several models (Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, K Nearest Neighbors, Support Vector Classifier, and Hierarchical Clustering) in our analysis and ultimately found unsupervised models did a better job of predicting whether a meteorite had been seen falling than supervised models. Linear regression was basically worthless and logistic regression performed better but still not to the level of some other models. We had better success with SVC and Hierarchical Clustering but our strongest performing model was K Nearest Neighbors, with a high degree of accuracy starting at about 5 k-neighbors.
    Feature Importance
    We used a Random Forest model to identify key features of meteorites seen falling and found Longitude, Elevation, Year, & Mass to be the largest overall predictors. Longitude is interesting as globally, there appear to be several hotspots that skew meteorite finds. The United States seems to have a relatively high proportion of meteorites centered around the Southwestern states. Meteorite finds have accelerated over time, likely due to population increase and better knowledge of what constitutes a meteorite. Mass also popped in terms of feature importance, which makes sense as only the largest meteors can even leave a meteorite behind. We did expect meteorite composition and land cover type (forest, shrubland, bare earth, etc.) to have a larger predictive effect than they did. A lot of the research we did in the lead up to our analysis indicated that deserts and farmland were ideal locations for finding meteorites, but we were unable to validate this with our analysis.
